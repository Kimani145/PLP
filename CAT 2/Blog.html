Real-Life Stories of Resilience

Amid the challenges posed by gender-based violence (GBV), stories of resilience and courage offer hope and demonstrate the power of community, faith, and action. These individuals and groups, from survivors to activists, remind us that change is possible, even in the darkest moments.

1. Makka’s Journey

Makka Kassim, a mother of six and a survivor of Female Genital Mutilation (FGM) and forced child marriage, is a living testament to the power of resilience and advocacy. After enduring the trauma of FGM at just seven years old, followed by the challenges of being married off at 16, Makka began questioning the harmful traditions upheld by her community.

Despite being accused of witchcraft and ostracized, Makka persevered. She founded the Kamuthe Women’s Network in 2012, which has since grown to over 2,500 members, advocating against FGM and other forms of GBV. Through dialogue with cultural and religious leaders, Makka and her team have shifted perspectives, gained community support, and empowered survivors with education and leadership skills.

Makka’s husband, who initially opposed her efforts, became a champion for her cause, rallying other men to join the fight against FGM. Together, they have created safe shelters, educated survivors, and inspired societal change in Kenya’s Garissa County.

Source: ActionAid Ireland https://actionaid.ie/makkas-story/

2. Cecilia’s Triumph

In 2007, during Kenya’s post-election violence, Cecilia was gang-raped by six men on her way home from work. Ostracized by her partner and left without support, she faced overwhelming despair. A few months later, she discovered she was pregnant. Though she initially considered abortion, her mother encouraged her to keep the baby.

Years later, while battling intense hatred for her daughter and contemplating suicide, Cecilia met a woman who introduced her to a community-based organization. Through counseling and financial empowerment programs, she slowly began to heal. Today, Cecilia runs a small business, loves her daughter deeply, and advocates for grassroots funding to support GBV survivors in her community.

Source: https://nation.africa/kenya/news/gender/my-gbv-story-four-slum-women-speak-out-4445732

3. Judith’s Redemption

At just 16, Judith, a schoolgirl from Nairobi, fell prey to a trusted friend who manipulated and defiled her. After becoming pregnant, she faced rejection from her father and dropped out of school. Consumed by shame and fear, Judith isolated herself until a social worker reached out.

With counseling and community support, she began to rebuild her confidence. Today, Judith is focused on providing a better future for her daughter and urges young girls to avoid falling for peer pressure. Her story highlights the importance of psychosocial support and education in helping survivors reclaim their lives.

Empowering Survivors: Steps to Healing

Supporting survivors of gender-based violence (GBV) requires more than compassion—it demands actionable solutions that help them heal, rebuild their lives, and regain their confidence. Through trauma-informed care, economic empowerment, and faith-based support, survivors can find the strength to overcome their pain:

1. Trauma-Informed Care

The emotional and psychological scars of GBV can be deep, but trauma-informed care prioritizes safety and healing for survivors.

Counseling Services: Professional counseling helps survivors process their trauma and reclaim their mental well-being.

Safe Spaces: Shelters like the Kamuthe Women’s Network Safe House provide sanctuary for survivors fleeing FGM and forced marriages.

Support Groups: Peer groups allow survivors to share their stories and find strength in collective healing.

“He heals the brokenhearted and binds up their wounds.” (Psalm 147:3)
“Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted.” (Matthew 5:4)

These verses remind survivors that God sees their pain and offers healing and comfort through faith and support.

2. Economic Empowerment

Economic independence is critical for survivors, as it allows them to leave abusive situations and rebuild their lives.

Skills Training and Education: Programs teaching practical skills empower survivors to earn a living.

Microloans and Entrepreneurship: Small loans enable survivors to start businesses, such as Cecilia’s charcoal and detergent business, which helped her reclaim stability after post-election violence.

Reintegration into Education: Adolescents like Judith, who dropped out of school due to abuse, need scholarships and educational opportunities to restore their futures.

“The work of their hands He blesses, and His blessings are upon those who labor with love.” (Ecclesiasticus 14:20).


3. Legal Protection and Justice

Justice systems must be accessible, survivor-centered, and efficient to ensure that survivors feel safe and heard.

Survivor-Friendly Courtrooms: Private chambers and virtual testimonies protect survivors from re-traumatization.

Free Legal Aid: Legal support ensures survivors can access justice regardless of financial means.

Enforcement of Laws: Strict enforcement of anti-GBV laws is vital to preventing further harm and ensuring accountability.

“Seek justice, rescue the oppressed, defend the orphan, plead for the widow.” (Isaiah 1:17)

“The Lord executes righteousness and judgment for all who are oppressed.” (Psalm 103:6)

These verses emphasize God’s demand for justice, encouraging society to fight for the rights of the oppressed and to stand against injustice

4. Community-Based Support

Communities play an essential role in breaking the cycle of GBV by challenging harmful practices and supporting survivors.

Awareness Campaigns: Educating communities about GBV’s impact is key to changing perceptions and reducing stigma.

Engaging Religious and Cultural Leaders: Leaders have the power to challenge harmful norms and encourage positive change.

Survivor Support Networks: Chamas and savings groups provide financial and emotional support to survivors.

The Story of St. Maria Goretti: A Model of Forgiveness and Courage

At the young age of 11, St. Maria Goretti resisted an attempted assault by her neighbor, Alessandro Serenelli. Despite her fear, Maria courageously pleaded with him to respect her dignity and not commit a grave sin. In a violent rage, Alessandro stabbed Maria multiple times.

While on her deathbed, Maria forgave her attacker, saying:

I forgive Alessandro Serenelli, and I want him to be with me in heaven.

Her act of forgiveness profoundly impacted Alessandro, who later repented, turned to faith, and lived the rest of his life as a man devoted to God. Maria’s story, canonized by the Church, inspires survivors to find strength in their faith and shows that forgiveness, while challenging, can bring profound peace.

The Role of Society in prevention

Preventing gender-based violence (GBV) is not just the responsibility of survivors or authorities—it’s a shared duty that involves everyone. Communities, families, educators, and leaders all play a crucial role in breaking harmful norms, supporting survivors, and creating a culture of respect and equality.

1. Breaking Harmful Norms and Stereotypes

Many acts of GBV are rooted in societal beliefs that normalize inequality and violence. Challenging these norms is essential.

Confronting Stereotypes: Replace phrases like “Boys don’t cry” or “A girl cannot do it alone” with affirmations such as “Everyone deserves respect and opportunity.”

Community Conversations: Organize forums where people can discuss the impact of harmful traditions like FGM, forced marriages, and domestic violence.

“Do not conform to the pattern of this world, but be transformed by the renewing of your mind.” (Romans 12:2)

2. Educating Youth

Education is a powerful tool for change. Teaching children about gender equality and mutual respect can shape future generations to reject violence.

School Programs: Introduce lessons on consent, emotional intelligence, and healthy relationships into school curriculums.

Early Intervention: Work with youth to identify and address problematic behaviors, such as bullying or toxic masculinity, before they escalate into violence.

Promoting Role Models: Showcase stories of men and women who exemplify equality and mutual respect.

“Train up a child in the way he should go, and when he is old he will not depart from it.” (Proverbs 22:6)

3. Engaging Men and Boys as Allies

Men and boys are critical allies in the fight against GBV. By challenging harmful behaviors and supporting survivors, they can become champions for change.

Programs for Men: Encourage men to join initiatives like Makka Kassim’s husband’s Men’s Network Against FGM, which promotes gender equality and rejects harmful practices.

Community Role Models: Empower male leaders to advocate against GBV, demonstrating that real strength lies in respect and compassion.

“Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave Himself up for her.” (Ephesians 5:25)

4. The Role of Faith Communities

Faith communities hold immense influence in promoting values of dignity and respect.

Religious Leaders as Advocates: Encourage imams, pastors, and priests to speak out against GBV during sermons and community events.

Incorporating Spiritual Teachings: Highlight verses from holy texts that emphasize justice and compassion.

“Allah commands you to render trusts to whom they are due and when you judge between people to judge with justice.” (Quran 4:58)
“Love your neighbor as yourself.” (Mark 12:31)

5. Community Support Systems

Communities are the first line of defense against GBV. A strong support network can prevent violence and help survivors heal.

Creating Safe Spaces: Establish shelters and resource centers where survivors can find safety and support.

Encouraging Bystander Intervention: Train community members to recognize and safely intervene in situations of abuse or harassment.

1. Technology-Facilitated GBV: A Growing Concern

With the rise of digital platforms, new forms of GBV have emerged, often referred to as technology-facilitated GBV. These include:

Cyberbullying and Online Harassment: Women and girls are disproportionately targeted through abusive messages, threats, and trolling.

Non-Consensual Sharing of Intimate Images: Often referred to as “revenge porn,” this form of violence aims to humiliate and intimidate victims.

Online Stalking and Surveillance: Abusers use technology to monitor and control their victims’ activities.

“Verily, Allah does not wrong the people at all, but it is the people who are unjust to themselves.” (Quran 10:44)

7. AI and Its Role in Combating GBV

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is reshaping how we address gender-based violence (GBV), acting as both a tool for progress and, at times, a weapon for harm. By harnessing AI responsibly, we can amplify efforts to prevent violence, support survivors, and create safer communities.

1. AI as a Tool to Combat GBV

AI is revolutionizing how we respond to GBV in several ways:

Survivor Support:

AI-powered chatbots like Sophie Bot in Kenya provide survivors with immediate, confidential support, offering guidance on safety plans, rights, and resources.

Apps like Woebot use AI to help survivors manage trauma and improve their mental health.

Detection and Monitoring of Online GBV:

AI algorithms help social media platforms detect and remove harmful content, including cyberbullying, harassment, and threats.

Tools like PhotoDNA identify and block the spread of intimate images shared without consent, combating "revenge porn."

Data-Driven Prevention:

Governments and NGOs use AI to analyze trends in GBV, identify high-risk areas, and allocate resources effectively.

AI models provide insights into the effectiveness of laws and programs, enabling evidence-based policymaking.

Enhancing Legal Processes:

Tools like Truepic ensure that digital evidence remains untampered, strengthening survivor cases in court.

AI-powered transcription services reduce survivor trauma by recording statements for legal use without requiring repeated recounting of painful events.

2. AI Misuse in Perpetuating GBV

While AI has immense potential for good, it has also been misused in ways that harm survivors:

Deepfake Abuse:

Perpetrators use AI-generated deepfake videos to create non-consensual explicit content, often for blackmail or public humiliation.

Victims face stigma and struggle to prove the content is fake, causing emotional and reputational harm.

Surveillance and Stalking:

Abusers misuse AI-powered tools like location trackers, spyware, and even smart home devices to monitor and control survivors.

Automated Harassment:

AI bots can be programmed to send abusive messages or flood survivors’ social media accounts, amplifying the psychological impact of harassment.

3. Bridging the Gaps: Challenges in Ethical AI Use

Bias in AI Systems:

Many AI models are trained on biased datasets, which may ignore or misidentify abuse against marginalized groups.

Lack of Regulation:

Global laws have yet to fully address AI misuse in GBV, leaving gaps in protecting survivors from digital harm.

Digital Literacy Gaps:

Many survivors lack knowledge about protecting themselves from AI-driven threats like deepfakes or tracking.

4. The Way Forward: Harnessing AI Responsibly

To maximize AI’s potential while minimizing risks, we must:

Strengthen AI for Good:

Invest in AI tools that enhance survivor safety, such as real-time abuse detection and location-based support apps.

Encourage partnerships between tech companies and GBV organizations to develop survivor-focused solutions.

Increase Digital Literacy:

Educate communities, especially women and girls, on how to protect their digital presence and recognize AI-driven threats.

Advocate for Regulations:

Push for laws criminalizing AI misuse in GBV, such as deepfake creation and digital stalking.

Ensure that tech platforms enforce clear, transparent policies against GBV-related abuse.

Promote Ethical AI Development:

AI developers must prioritize inclusivity and fairness, addressing the unique challenges faced by diverse communities impacted by GBV.
